Chapter 1
Reading the Signal Banner

The coloured banner at the top of each instrument card is the most important thing on the page. It tells you immediately whether you should be buying, selling, watching, or waiting.

▲ BUY NOW Price is inside the demand (buy) zone right now. This is when to enter a BUY trade. Use the entry price shown and set your stop loss and take profit levels exactly as displayed.
▼ SELL NOW Price is inside the supply (sell) zone right now. This is when to enter a SELL trade. Use the entry price shown and set your stop loss and take profit levels exactly as displayed.
WATCH · ZONE NEAR Price is approaching the zone but not inside it yet. Prepare your trade in advance. Set a limit order at the entry price shown so it triggers automatically when price arrives.
WAIT · NO SETUP Price is far from the zone. Do nothing. There is no active trade setup. Chasing price away from the zone is how most traders lose money — patience is the strategy here.
Key rule Only trade when the banner says BUY NOW or SELL NOW. The signal refreshes every time you click Refresh or reload the page.

Below the signal banner you'll see five levels: Entry, Stop Loss, TP1, TP2, and TP3. These are your exact trade levels — no guessing required.

Chapter 2
Market Structure — HH, HL, LH, LL

Markets don't move in straight lines. Price swings up to a peak (swing high), then pulls back to a trough (swing low), then swings again. By labelling each peak and trough, we can identify whether the market is trending up, trending down, or going sideways.

▲ Uptrend (Bullish)

Each swing high is Higher than the last (HH = Higher High) and each swing low is also Higher than the last (HL = Higher Low). This means buyers are in control and we look for BUY opportunities.

▼ Downtrend (Bearish)

Each swing high is Lower than the last (LH = Lower High) and each swing low is also Lower than the last (LL = Lower Low). Sellers are in control and we look for SELL opportunities.

LabelMeaningSignals
HHHigher High — new peak above the previous peakBullish continuation
HLHigher Low — pullback that holds above the previous lowBuyers defending lows — bullish
LHLower High — rally that fails below the previous peakSellers capping price — bearish
LLLower Low — new trough below the previous troughBearish continuation
Tip The signal conviction shows HIGH when the last two structure points both agree on direction (e.g. HH + HL together = strong bullish). MODERATE means the signals are mixed.
Chapter 3
Order Blocks

An order block is a specific candle (or group of candles) where a large institution — a bank or hedge fund — placed a significant buy or sell order. Price often returns to these levels because the institution still has unfilled orders waiting there.

Demand / Bullish Order Block

The last bearish (down) candle before a strong upward move that breaks a recent swing high. When price pulls back to this level, institutions re-enter long (buy) positions. This is a high-probability buy zone.

Supply / Bearish Order Block

The last bullish (up) candle before a strong downward move that breaks a recent swing low. When price rallies back to this level, institutions re-enter short (sell) positions. This is a high-probability sell zone.

How to use it When a BUY NOW signal appears and there is a bullish order block below or at the current price, the signal has extra confirmation. More order blocks = higher conviction.
Chapter 4
Fair Value Gap (FVG)

A Fair Value Gap is a price imbalance — a gap between three consecutive candles where no trading took place. Think of it as a "hole" in the market. Price is naturally drawn back to fill these gaps because the market seeks balance (fair value).

Bullish FVG

The high of candle 1 is lower than the low of candle 3 — there is a gap between them. This gap acts as support. When price pulls back into it, it is a potential BUY area.

Bearish FVG

The low of candle 1 is higher than the high of candle 3. This gap acts as resistance. When price rallies back into it, it is a potential SELL area.

The dashboard shows how many FVGs are unfilled (price has not yet returned to them). A high count of unfilled bullish FVGs below price supports a bullish bias — the market has a lot of unfinished business below.

Filled vs Unfilled A filled FVG means price has already traded back through the gap. Filled gaps no longer act as support or resistance. The app only counts unfilled ones.
Chapter 5
Inverse Fair Value Gap (IFVG)

An Inverse FVG (IFVG) is a Fair Value Gap that has been broken — price closed completely through it — and has therefore flipped its role. A bullish FVG that gets violated becomes a bearish IFVG (now acts as resistance). A bearish FVG that gets violated becomes a bullish IFVG (now acts as support).

IFVGs appear on the dashboard as a count. A high IFVG count means the market has repeatedly swept through imbalances, which signals that institutions are actively repositioning — often a sign of strong momentum or a significant trend change.

Note IFVGs are an advanced concept. If you're new to trading, focus on the BUY/SELL signal, entry price, stop loss, and take profit levels first.
Chapter 6
Trendline

The trendline is drawn by connecting the most recent swing lows (in an uptrend) or swing highs (in a downtrend) using linear regression — a mathematical line of best fit through those points.

The dashboard shows the projected trendline price — where the trendline sits right now. If the current price is near the trendline, it adds confirmation to a trade signal. A bullish setup is stronger when price is bouncing off an ascending (rising) trendline.

Confluence When the buy zone, an order block, an FVG, and the trendline all sit at roughly the same price level, that is called confluence. Confluence significantly increases the probability of a successful trade.
Chapter 7
Fibonacci Levels

Fibonacci retracement levels are horizontal lines drawn between the most recent significant swing low and swing high (or vice versa). They show where price is likely to retrace (pull back) before continuing in the original direction.

LevelNameMeaning
0.38238.2% retracementShallow pullback — market is very strong
0.50050% retracementMid-point equilibrium — common support/resistance
0.618 ★61.8% — Golden RatioStart of golden pocket — high probability reversal zone
0.786 ★78.6% retracementEnd of golden pocket — deep but still valid pullback
1.272 extExtension level 1First target beyond the swing high — used for TP2
1.618 extExtension level 2Full extension — used for TP3 target

The Golden Pocket (61.8%–78.6%) is the most powerful retracement zone. When price pulls back into this range and aligns with an order block or FVG, it produces one of the highest-probability trade entries in smart money trading.

Chapter 8
Buy / Sell Zone

The buy or sell zone is the price range where the app expects institutions to step in and reverse price. It is built from the confluence of the nearest order block, unfilled FVG, and fibonacci golden pocket — all pointing in the same direction.

The zone is always at least 50 pips wide. This ensures you have room to set a limit order inside the zone without being too precise. You don't need to catch the exact low — trading within the zone is enough.

Buy Zone (Demand)

Located below the current price. Wait for price to fall into this range, then buy. The entry price shown is the midpoint of the zone. Stop loss goes 80 pips below entry.

Sell Zone (Supply)

Located above the current price. Wait for price to rise into this range, then sell. The entry price shown is the midpoint of the zone. Stop loss goes 80 pips above entry.

Chapter 9
Trade Plan — Entry, Stop Loss, Take Profits

Every signal comes with a complete trade plan. Here is what each level means and how to use it:

1
Entry Price
The midpoint of the buy or sell zone. Place a limit order at this price — meaning your broker will only fill the trade if price actually reaches this level. Never buy or sell at market just because a signal appeared; wait for price to come to the zone.
2
Stop Loss (SL) — Fixed 80 Pips
The stop loss is placed 80 pips below entry (for buys) or 80 pips above entry (for sells). If price reaches the stop loss, the trade closes automatically and your loss is limited. For gold, 80 pips = $8.00 per 0.01 lot. Never trade without a stop loss.
3
TP1 — First Take Profit
The nearest swing point in the direction of the trade. This is the closest realistic target. When price reaches TP1, consider closing 50% of your position and moving your stop loss to break-even (your entry price) to protect your profit.
4
TP2 — Second Take Profit
The next structural level beyond TP1. Close another 30% of your position here. This typically gives a 2–3× risk-to-reward ratio (shown as R on the dashboard). A 2.5R trade means you make 2.5× more than you risked.
5
TP3 — Final Take Profit
The maximum target based on the 1.618 Fibonacci extension of the swing. Close the remaining 20% of your position here. TP3 represents the best-case outcome and is not always reached — always secure TP1 and TP2 first.
Position sizing suggestion Enter with your full intended size at the entry price. At TP1: close 50%, move stop to break-even. At TP2: close 30% of original. Let the final 20% run to TP3.
Chapter 10
Risk Management

Technical signals increase probability — they do not guarantee profit. Every trade carries risk. These rules protect your account:

The 1% Rule

Never risk more than 1–2% of your total account on a single trade. If your account is $1,000, your maximum loss per trade should be $10–$20. Size your position so that hitting the stop loss costs only that amount.

Wait for Confirmation

Always verify the signal on your own charting platform (TradingView, MT4, etc.) before entering. These signals are generated from daily candles — allow 1 to 5 trading sessions for price to reach the zone.

Daily Candles Only

All analysis uses the daily timeframe. Do not trade the signal on a 1-minute or 5-minute chart — the zones are too wide. Use the daily or 4-hour chart to confirm and entry the trade.

Demo First

If you are new to trading, practice on a demo account for at least 4 weeks before using real money. Get comfortable with placing limit orders, stop losses, and take profits before risking capital.
